# Project Presentation: Artificial Intelligence Current Event Topics

## Objective

Students will research and present a recent or ongoing current event related to artificial intelligence (AI).

This project has **three main goals**:

1. **Learn** about a real AI topic happening in the world right now
2. **Use AI** throughout your project — not just to help with research, but also to have AI evaluate and grade your own finished work
3. **Reflect** on the experience — what did it feel like to be graded by AI? Did you agree with it? What did AI do well, and where did it fall short?

This is a hands-on project about AI, *using* AI. You are expected to think critically about the tools you are using — not just accept whatever AI tells you.

## Part 1: AI Evaluation — Ask AI to Grade Your Work

### What You Need to Do

After your group has finished the project, you will submit your work to **two different AI models** and ask each one to evaluate your project and suggest a grade.

This is not just about getting feedback. It is about experiencing what it is like to have AI assess your work — and then deciding for yourself whether you agree.

---

### Step-by-Step Instructions

**Step 1 — Choose 2 AI Models**

Your group picks any two AI assistants. Some options:

- ChatGPT (OpenAI) — [chatgpt.com](https://chatgpt.com)
- Claude (Anthropic) — [claude.ai](https://claude.ai)
- Gemini (Google) — [gemini.google.com](https://gemini.google.com)
- Copilot (Microsoft) — [copilot.microsoft.com](https://copilot.microsoft.com)
- Any other AI assistant your group prefers

You may use any two — there is no required combination.

---

**Step 2 — Write Your Prompt**

Write a prompt that gives the AI:

- Your GitHub repository link
- Your YouTube video link
- The grading rubric (copy it from the Sample Rubric section below)
- A clear request: you want the AI to evaluate your project and suggest a grade for each category

You are writing this prompt yourself. Use the sample below as a starting point, but make it your own. A more specific and well-written prompt usually gets more useful feedback.

---

**Step 3 — Save Your Prompts and AI Responses**

- Copy your full prompt and paste it into a document
- Copy the AI's full response and paste it below your prompt
- Do this for **both AI models**
- Save both in your repository under `/ai-evaluation/`

Suggested file names:
```
/ai-evaluation/ai-model-1.md   ← Prompt + response from first AI
/ai-evaluation/ai-model-2.md   ← Prompt + response from second AI
```

> ⚠️ **Important:** You must save the actual prompts you used, not just the AI responses. Your instructor needs to see exactly what you asked.

---

**Step 4 — Write Your Reflection**

After reviewing what both AIs said, write your group's reflection. See Part 2 below for the required structure.

---

### Sample Prompt Template

Use this as a **starting point only**. You are expected to write your own version and adjust it as needed.

```
You are a college instructor evaluating a student group project for a basic 
computer science course about artificial intelligence.

Please review the project at the links below and suggest a grade using the 
rubric I have provided. Give me a score for each category, an overall 
suggested grade, specific strengths, and areas to improve.

**GitHub Repository:** [paste your link here]
**YouTube Video:** [paste your YouTube link here — make sure it is public]

---

Grading Rubric (each category is worth 25 points, total out of 100):

1. Content & Presentation (25 points)
   - Is the AI topic recent (within the last 6–12 months)?
   - Is the information accurate and well-explained in simple, clear language?
   - Is the video organized and within the 2:30–3:00 minute time limit?
   - Does the group explain why this AI topic matters to society?
   - Does the video end with a thoughtful discussion question?
   - Do all team members participate in the video?

2. GitHub Collaboration (25 points)
   - Is the repository organized and easy to navigate?
   - Did all team members make commits?
   - Are GitHub Issues, branches, and pull requests used?
   - Does the README contain all required information (team names, topic 
     summary, YouTube link, discussion question, sources)?
   - Is the Discussion board used for team communication or peer responses?

3. AI Evaluation Setup (25 points)
   - Did the group submit to 2 different AI models?
   - Are the full prompts saved and visible in the repository?
   - Is the rubric included in the prompt so the AI has context?
   - Is the AI's full response saved alongside the prompt?
   - Is the prompt specific and thoughtful, or is it vague and generic?

4. Reflection Quality (25 points)
   - Does the reflection compare the two AI grading results?
   - Does the group share a clear opinion about whether they agree 
     with the AI's suggested grade?
   - Does the group reflect on how AI helped or did not help 
     during their research?
   - Is the reflection honest and specific, with real examples?

---

Please provide:
- A score out of 25 for each of the 4 categories above
- A total score out of 100
- 2–3 specific strengths of this project
- 2–3 specific areas that could be improved
- Any other comments you think are useful
```

> 💡 **Tip:** You can make your prompt better by adding more context — for example, telling the AI what level the course is, who the audience is, or what specific parts of the project you want feedback on. Experimenting with your prompt is part of the assignment.

## Part 2: Reflection Document

Your group must write a reflection and save it as `reflection.md` in your repository root.

**Use the exact structure below.** Your instructor will use this structure when grading your reflection. Missing sections will affect your grade.

---

### Part A — AI Grading Reflection

*What happened when you asked AI to grade your project?*

---

**A1. AI Model 1 — Summary**

- Which AI model did you use? (name and version if known)
- What score did it suggest for each category?
- What were its main strengths comments?
- What did it say you should improve?

---

**A2. AI Model 2 — Summary**

- Which AI model did you use? (name and version if known)
- What score did it suggest for each category?
- What were its main strengths comments?
- What did it say you should improve?

---

**A3. Comparison — What Was Similar? What Was Different?**

- Where did the two AI models agree?
- Where did they disagree?
- Did one AI give more useful or detailed feedback than the other? Why do you think that is?

---

**A4. Do You Agree With the AI's Grade?**

- Do you think the AI's suggested grade is fair? Why or why not?
- Was there anything the AI said that surprised you?
- Was there anything the AI got wrong, missed, or misunderstood about your project?

---

**A5. What Would You Do Differently?**

- Based on what the AI told you, is there anything you would change if you could redo the project?
- Is there anything the AI suggested that you think is NOT worth changing? Explain why.

---

### Part B — AI Research Assistance Reflection

*How did AI help you (or not help you) while you were working on this project?*

---

**B1. How You Used AI During Research**

- Which AI tools did you use during the research phase? (e.g., ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot, Perplexity, etc.)
- What specific tasks did you use AI for? (e.g., finding facts, summarizing news articles, brainstorming topic ideas, writing or editing a draft, making slides, preparing questions)

---

**B2. What Worked Well**

- What was the most useful thing an AI did for your group?
- Can you give a specific example? (What did you ask? What did it give you that was actually helpful?)

---

**B3. What Did Not Work Well**

- Were there any times the AI gave you incorrect, outdated, or unhelpful information?
- What did you have to double-check, correct, or do yourself instead?
- Give at least one specific example.

---

**B4. Overall Reflection**

- How did using AI change the way your group worked on this project?
- If you did a similar project next time, would you use AI the same way? Would you do anything differently?
- What is the most important thing this experience taught you about AI as a tool?

## GitHub Best Practices

Use the following structure for your repository:

```
/research-notes          ← Your research and notes
/presentation-materials  ← Slides, scripts, outlines
/code-demos              ← Code examples (if applicable)
/media-assets            ← Images, diagrams (if applicable)
/ai-evaluation           ← Your prompts and AI responses (both models)
README.md                ← Project overview, YouTube link, discussion question
SOURCES.md               ← All references and sources used
reflection.md            ← Your group's reflection document (Parts A and B)
```

**Your `README.md` should include:**
- Project title and all team member names and student numbers
- A short summary of your AI topic
- Key findings from your research
- YouTube video link — **must be set to PUBLIC**
- Your final discussion question
- Links to your AI evaluation files
- All sources and references

**GitHub habits to follow:**
- Every team member must commit to the repository — commits are how we see who contributed
- Use **Issues** to assign tasks and track what needs to be done
- Use **branches** when working on different sections, then merge with a **Pull Request**
- Use the **Discussion board** for team communication and for responding to other groups
- Write clear commit messages (e.g., `Added research notes on EU AI Act` — not just `update`)

## Suggested AI Current Event Topics

### Technical Developments
- New large language model releases (GPT, Claude, Gemini, Llama, etc.)
- Multimodal AI — models that can see, hear, and read at the same time
- AI hardware and specialized chips (e.g., Nvidia, Apple Silicon, Google TPU)
- Open-source AI models and their effect on the industry

### Industry & Business
- AI in healthcare, education, or finance
- Major AI company mergers, acquisitions, or funding rounds
- AI tools in the workplace — productivity, automation, and job changes
- AI-powered products or services changing everyday life

### Ethical & Social Issues
- AI bias in hiring, loan approvals, or the justice system
- Deepfakes and AI-generated misinformation
- AI-generated content and copyright law
- How AI is changing jobs and the workforce

### Regulation & Policy
- AI laws and regulations (EU AI Act, US executive orders)
- International AI safety summits and agreements
- AI in elections and political campaigns
- Data privacy rules and how they affect AI

### Research & Safety
- AI alignment research — making sure AI does what humans intend
- Explainable AI — understanding how AI makes decisions
- Emerging AI risks and safety measures

## Additional Tips

- **Be honest in your reflection.** If the AI gave you a lower grade than you expected — or said something you think is wrong — that is fine. Say so, and explain why you disagree. Honest critical thinking is worth more than simply agreeing with the AI.
- **Pick reputable sources.** Academic papers, official company announcements, and established news publications (BBC, Reuters, Wired, MIT Technology Review, etc.) are preferred. Include all sources in `SOURCES.md`.
- **Focus on recent events.** Your topic should come from the last 6–12 months.
- **Go beyond the technology.** Talk about the impact on people, jobs, society, or policy — not just how the technology works.
- **Set your YouTube video to PUBLIC** before you submit. Double-check this. If the video cannot be viewed, it cannot be graded.
- **All collaboration must be on GitHub.** If your team met on Discord or WhatsApp to plan tasks, that is fine — but the actual task tracking, decisions, and discussions must be documented on GitHub using Issues and the Discussion board.
